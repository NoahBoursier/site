Caster Wheel Life Test Fixture

Work Experience - Omron Robotics

In order to predict maintenance needs and life time of the wheels of robots, test fixtures are used to simulate constant use under worst case conditions. I was tasked with creating a test fixture that could do this with a variety of caster wheels. After seeing a previous design that used many custom manufactured components and was difficult to use and maintain, I decided to optimize my design. This design is extremely quick and easy to create, with only 4 simple custom parts, (I was able to build one in just one day!), everything else is composed of standard parts.

On the right is a CAD model for this design followed by one of the completed fixtures. As of writing this they’ve been running for 5 months.

Portable Light-Based Biosensor for Food Samples

Research - Purdue

As part of my graduate research I developed a device that could detect extremely low levels of light using a silicon photomultiplier sensor and a camera. The device will accompany samples being sent from food processing plants to the Food Safety Inspection Service (FSIS, part of the USDA) to monitor them for bacteria (E. coli O157:H7) in transit. A bacteriophage is introduced with the sample that would insert lux genes into the bacteria so that it produces light that the sensor can detect.

I used a laser with a series of optical density filters to test light sensitivity of my device as I tuned it. The images to the right are of the latest iteration of the device, with the external shell on and off. A lens focuses light onto the sensor and the light travels through a diffusive cone to increase the odds that wandering photons eventually find the sensor.

3-Phase Axial Flux Motor

Personal Project

I created a working, 900KV (this refers to a relationship between volts and revolutions) 3 phase axial flux motor. It has 24 magnets and 18 coils oriented with their magnetic fields parallel to the shaft, hence axial flux. The parts are all 3D printed in PLA except for some screws, threaded inserts, magnets, and copper which was coiled on a coil winder I designed for this motor. I met up with a friend who had a 3-phase motor controller and encoder to test it.

I designed and put this motor together in 24hrs, (not including the time it took to make the coil winder). I will never make another motor like this because it requires extensive soldering to assemble.

Mechatronic Hand

Personal Project

A cable driven biomimetic hand that I've ben working on in my free time. It's mostly 3D printed and is designed with a focus on being compact and easy to assemble. I didn't want to be struggling to run the cables through and tension them so I built in screw actuated crimps to hold the cables into place. I aim for the majority of the actuators to be located in the forearm, and so the cables are routed in such a way that their length is not dependent on the joints upstream. This is accomplished by ensuring cables always pass through the center of rotation.

Motor Coil Winder

Personal Project

In order to create my custom 3-Phase Axial motor I needed to create coils. I wanted them to be uniform and so I designed and fabricated this coil winder. I designed it with ease of assembly and use in mind. In order to wind a coil the user just has to insert a blank, attach the wire by running it through a little hook and clamping the 3D printed thumbscrew down. Then they can push a button and the machine begins to coil. It uses two stepper motors - one to turn the spindle with the coil's core which pulls wire on, and a second that controls where the wire is being layed onto the coil. It does this by turning a leadscrew to linearly move the feeder along the coil's length. All components except for the interfaces between the motors and the assembly are 3D printed and snap fit.

Temperature Controlled Plate For Biological Sample

Research - Purdue

Using a Peltier TEC (Thermo-Electric Cooler) is usually a bad idea. They are not very efficient when it comes to cooling long term, but they are excellent when used as a heating element. As part of my undergraduate research I created a temperature controlled heating device for a biological sample that used one such TEC to reach a desired temperature (65C) in a few seconds. Upon reaching that temperature it would maintain it within half a degree Celcius. This was achieved using an Arduino Uno, thermocouple, and mosfet to provided power to the TEC.

Buzzer Prank Device (Custom Etched PCB)

Course Project - Digital Electronics

As part of a high school digital electronics course we had the opportunity to design and create our own printed circuit boards (PCBs). We did it in person using a chemical etching process in the classroom after printing out our designs and transfering the ink to the a copper plated piece of FR4 (fiberglass circuit board material). My circuit used two 555 timers, capcitors, resistors, a buzzer, an LED, and a switch. The user would flip the switch and the device would wait 10 seconds before starting to beep and flash repeatedly. You might notice that my design is extremely compact. This is a common theme for me. I love optimizing for space efficiency.

You can see the etching process go from a printed page, ink transferred to copper coated FR4, and finally the etched PCB. Then I drilled holes and soldered my components in.
